4-Wheel Drive (4WD) Skid-Steer Layout: 

 Achieves turning by creating a speed difference between two wheels each on the left and right sides  

Each of the four wheels has their own independent motor OR 

Two individual, more powerful motor can each power one side via a chain or a belt  

Difficulty – Easy 

No pre-requisite calculation required, purely alignment by eye 

Wiring is simple 

Price – Low (£10) * 

 

6-Wheel Rocker-Bogie Layout: 

 

Most mars-rover adjacent wheel design  

Exceptionally well-designed for obstacle climbing and stability  

Most variations allow for all six wheels to have their own turning points 

One bogie on each side, connecting to two wheels, both of which have the same pivot point 

Rocker connected to the front wheel and the bogie, which is forced in the opposite direction of the bogie due to the differential force.  

Difficulty – High 

Expense – High (£30) 

 

Mecanum Wheel Layout (Omnidirectional) 

Uses specialised omnidirectional wheels that allow a vehicle or robot to move in any direction 

Achieved because of small, freely rotating passive rollers set at a 45 degree angle along the wheel's circumference 

Despite advantages, they have notable drawbacks – heavy vibration, instability and poor shock absorption, which makes it not ideal for heavy payloads or fragile components 

Alternative – Omni Wheels 

Can turn without an axle, due to rollers along the circumference of the wheel perpendicular to the moving direction of the car  

Works best with a wheel in the centre facing perpendicular to the other four wheels, allowing for better side-movement 

Difficulty – Medium 

Expense – Very High (£75) 

 

Tri-Star Wheel Setup: 

Uses 3 wheels on each side forming a triangle, which enables for better obstacle climbing  

Code is incredibly simple, standard skid-steer programming 

Can be used in two different iterations:  

A single motor that moves the frame connecting the three wheels instead of spinning the wheels themselves. However, instead of smooth movement, the wheels will 'tumble' along the floor, raising chassis stability issues.  

A single central 'sun gear', attached directly to the motor shaft, which drives internal planet gears that spins all three wheels simultaneously at the same speed 

Difficulty – Medium  

Expense – Medium  

 

Tracks:  

Superior traction to the rest, can lock into the terrain  

Create a continuous, self-laying pathway that the robot drives on.  

Use idler wheels – small unpowered guide wheels placed at opposite ends. Keeps the track taut and aligned so it does not slip off during turns.  

Best option for tracks is skid-steer, which turn by powering the right/left track forward and the other track backwards 

 

Tracks + Wheels  

Uses two unique setups that swap around when required.  

Uses custom triangular track design in order to climb stairs and other severe obstacles  

Uses an additional skid-steer layout in order to turn and travel smooth surfaces without additional machinery  

Uses a linear actuator to lower the tracks into place, which feature a four sided narrow trapezoid shape, with one slanted side to help with stair and obstacle manoeuvring.  

Difficulty – Extreme  

Cost – Very High (£75)
